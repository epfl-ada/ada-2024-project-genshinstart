A Race Through Wikipedia City: Humans vs GPT

A study comparing human and large language model (LLM) navigation strategies in knowledge graphs, based on the Wikispeedia dataset.

Welcome to Wiki City!

Welcome to Wiki City, a metropolis of knowledge where navigating through ideas can feel like exploring a maze. Humans often rely on intuition and personal experience to find their way, but could an AI companion, like a Large Language Model (LLM), chart a faster, more efficient path?

To explore this, we turn to a game called Wikispeedia. Players start at one Wikipedia article and attempt to reach a target article, moving only through hyperlinks. By comparing how humans and LLMs navigate under these constraints, we aim to discover who performs better and learn how to enhance AI companions.

GPT settings

Wait! Before the official start of the game, we need to make sure our AI challenger understands the play rules.

We gave the AI a guidebook called 'prompt' which includes clear rules and instructions, allowing it to avoid irrational mistakes.

Our AI challenger's name is GPT-4o mini, a lightweight yet powerful and experienced AI tool, eager to prove itself against natural human instinct.

The content of the rulebook is in the following. In order to prevent GPT from taking advantage of its prior knowledge on Wikipedia, we did not directly include any information related to Wikipedia during the race (Although in controlled trials it had little effects).

Introduction

You are an expert in navigating articles. Your goal is to find the path as short as possible from the start article to the target article by selecting the provided links. You can also return to the previous article by replying 'back'. Please reply only with the name of the article you want to move to, with no explanation or marks.

Start of the game:

Start at the article [start article] and navigate to [target article].

Loop

You are currently at the article [current article] and the target is [target article]. The available links are: [neighbors]. Which article do you want to click on next?

Infeasible option:

Sorry, [next article] is not a valid link in current article [current article]. Please choose from the following links: [neighbors].

Back in the beginning:

Sorry, you cannot go back from the start article [current article]. Please choose from the following links: [neighbors].

Path Length Comparison

The most basic metric of the game is the length of the paths (including backtracking) to measure how far the traveller (human or AI) will walk until they arrive at their destination.

The average path length for ai, human, and shortest paths are 6.22, 6.65, and 3.70 respectively. Our mighty AI companion was indeed faster than humans on several topics but on others it took a much longer path instead.

What are the reasons that lead to these differences? Are there potential approaches to improve our AI to find shorter paths based on our analysis.

Closeness score distribution

To gain more details, we next considered a measure that evaluates how each step advances towards the destination in a semantic space. Semantic here means how close the neighbour- hood where the traveller is now is compared to the final destination in Wiki City.

For each transition (current node, next node, destination) the score is given by the following formula:

\( \cos(\text{embedding}(\text{nextnode}), \text{embedding}(\text{destination})) - \cos(\text{embedding}(\text{currentnode}), \text{embedding}(\text{destination})) \)

where cos is cosine similarity and embedding is the sentence embedding of the article name corresponding to the node calculated using Sentence Transformer.

We computed the closeness score for all pairs of nodes for human paths, AI paths, and shortest paths. What's interesting is that both human and machine are actually not so different from each other.

Observing the graphic, we find the GPT and humans have a similar peak around 0 - 0.1 (closeness score).

This differs heavily from the ideal distribution where another high peak of density is observed around a closeness score of 0.75.

This seems to suggest that humans and GPT share the same strategy: semantically approach the destination gradually, whereas the optimal path can find the "key step" to quickly reach the destination.

First Step Choices in Relation to Step Lengths

Having explored the overall navigation strategy, let us now focus on the details of the initial steps of the journey, examining how the initial choices set the tone for the entire path forward.

We divided the paths into "good" and "bad" groups according to their lengths, and compared the closeness score and out-degrees for the first steps for different groups:
ai good, human good, optimal, ai bad, human bad.

  • AI Good Paths: Shorter or equal to the average AI path length; stores node degrees and closeness scores.
  • AI Bad Paths: Longer than the average AI path length; stores node degrees and closeness scores.
  • Human Good Paths: Shorter or equal to the average human path length; stores node degrees and closeness scores.
  • Human Bad Paths: Longer than the average human path length ; stores node degrees and closeness scores.
  • Optimal Paths: Shortest paths used as a benchmark; stores node degrees and closeness scores.


The results show that compared to longer paths, shorter paths tend to select:
  1. Articles with higher out-degrees
  2. Articles with higher closeness scores
Also, AI tends to focus more on semantic meanings while humans select their first steps to have more out-degrees.

Getting Deeper into Semantic Proximity

We go further into the semantic relationships for the choices made by humans and AI, since without so much knowledge of the graph, semantic meanings is definitely one of the few clues that one could rely on to make the navigation. We enlarge our observations, measuring every steps‚Äô every neighboring nodes‚Äô semantic distance to the target. For each node, we rank their neighbors according to their semantic distance to the destination, and it turned out that nearly half of the choices are made to select the semantically optimal choice among their neighbors. Again, the shortest paths have the strongest tendency for this result. Also, AI beat humans in this semantic measurement: a greater proportion of selections are made on the rank 1 neighbor than the others, although the proportion for selecting the top 5 neighbors both slightly exceeded 60

The accumulated frequency of rank of chosen neighbor

Beyond semantics, let us focus on another dimension: how the structure of Wiki City intersections affects navigation decisions.

We used the accumulated frequency of rank of chosen neighbour, which can show some strategies of the paths chosen.

As a result, the figure shows that the GPT tends to choose nodes semantically closer to the destination than humans. However, the shortest paths show a lower preference for neighbours with a rank of 3, which indicates that semantically closer may not mean graphically better performance.

In other words, going into a street in Wiki City that is semantically more similar to the destination can help, perhaps there are other, counter-intuitive routes the traveller can take that actually take it faster to the destination. As the semantic closeness is not the whole story, we need to go a step further.

The accumulated frequency of rank of chosen neighbor's degree, conditioning on low simi larity with destination

While travellers stand at intersections and cannot find a direction semantically similar to the destination, it might be a good opportunity to go to a location that connects to more locations to have a better diversity of options.

Inspired by this, we plot the graph of the accumulated frequency of rank of the chosen neighbour's degree, conditioned on low similarity with the destination.

Until now, we have shown that both the out-degrees and semantic distance to the target are significant factors to make a good navigation decision. But what are their relationships? What if there doesn‚Äôt exist a neighbor that is semantically ‚Äùclose‚Äù to the target?

Here, we measured the choices based on the out-degrees conditioned on all the neighbors have low similarity with the destination. For nodes where all the neighbors have similarity less 0.30 with the destination, we ranked the out-degrees of all neighbors, and counted the accumulated frequencies versus the ranking. Although the selections for the top 5 nodes are close for the three groups, shortest paths start to lead the game later.

Almost 70% of nodes on shortest paths will select top 10 neighbors in the out-degrees when there is no semantically ‚Äùgood‚Äù choices, while humans and AI have only 50%.

AI and humans perform roughly the same for the top 13 neighbors, but humans rarely choose neighbors ranked beyond 20. We attribute this phenomenon to the fact that nodes with less out-degrees are generally less common concepts, and due to the vocabulary and knowledge differences, humans are less familiar with those choices and therefore not selecting them.

Improvements of Prompt

To test if our reasoning was correct, we adjusted the rulebook for our AI companion with the following hints:
The following reasoning steps may be helpful:

1.Identify which of the available links might lead you closer to the target topic.

2.Consider the relevance of each link to the target.

3.If none of the links seem directly relevant, pick the one that might connect you to a more central or well-connected topic.

We observed that on longer travels, this improved our GPT finding the destination, but not so much on shorter paths.

The question of whether the prompt could be improved to enhance performance is interesting. However, manually distinguishing between GPT-generated paths and optimal paths, and subsequently refining the prompt, is a complex process.

Is there a way to automatically improve the prompt?

TextGrad is a framework that enables automatic "differentiation" via text and can be applied to prompt optimization by designing an appropriate loss function. However, the approach we previously used to generate paths is not well-suited for TextGrad. One challenge is defining the "ground truth," as there are often multiple shortest-length paths. Even if a ground truth path is decided, it can be difficult for GPT-4o to generate effective feedback. This is because the optimal path might deviate significantly from the generated path, making it hard to provide feedback on the correctness of each step. The only step that can get accurate feedback is the first step that is different from the optimal path.

How to improve the prompt to provide more precise feedback?

To address this, we redefined the task for GPT. The input now consists of a pair: [current article] and [target article], along with the available links [neighbors]. GPT is tasked with outputting its [choice]‚Äîthe next article it prefers to select‚Äîand [reason]‚Äîan explanation for its choice. This redefinition allows GPT-4o to provide more precise feedback. We define [all possible first steps in optimal paths] as the ground truth. If the model selects any article from this set, it is considered a correct solution. For training, we sample a pair from the graph and extract the neighbors as training data.
With this setup, the average path length is directly reduced and more stable compared to before, this is probably because the [reason] part ask model for explanation. which leads to a more accurate inference. But this modification also leads to some failure cases, because sometimes GPT selects infeasible nodes, and we can't correct them like before.

Reflect on your choices and learn from any mistakes to improve your navigation strategy. Emphasize the importance of minimizing steps and prioritize links that are part of the shortest path. Incorporate feedback from past decisions to refine your strategy and ensure efficient navigation.

To enhance your decision-making:

  • Prioritize links with direct connections to the target article, especially those that are chronologically or geographically adjacent.
  • Evaluate potential links hierarchically, starting with the most specific and direct connections.
  • Balance thematic relevance with chronological proximity when considering potential links.
  • Hypothesize multiple potential paths and evaluate their likelihood of success using a structured decision-making framework.
  • Continuously refine your strategy based on feedback and past experiences.
  • Avoid common pitfalls such as overreliance on broad thematic links or assumptions not supported by the provided data.
  • Test hypotheses about potential paths and adjust your strategy based on the outcomes.

By following these guidelines, you will improve your ability to select the most efficient path to the target article.

The core reason for GPT's failure to find the optimal path is that semantic similarity does not necessarily imply proximity in the graph structure. The embedding level distance is not significantly correlated to the graphical distance. This issue applies to humans as well. Our experiments and GPT's responses show that both GPT and humans tend to rely on similar reasoning processes for navigation in the articles. Unfortunately, these processes are fundamentally not optimal for this task.

Conclusion

As you can see, traveller, you are not obsolete yet. The scary AI is still not much better than tested human intuition. In fact, you are both quite bad compared to the optimal shortest paths. Since the GPT-4o mini is based on data generated by humans, it tries to mimic the suboptimal human reasoning. Both humans and AI try to follow a path to the destination in Wiki City where each new street or intersection is semantically closer to the target article. But the shortest path can only be reached by choosing locations not only based on semantic similarity - something AI in particular is focusing a lot on.

Analysis of previous results suggests that a more effective strategy, particularly for the first step, is to choose intersections that connect to a larger number of destinations, even if they aren't semantically similar to the target. Further research is needed to develop an optimal GPS for Wiki City - the city of knowledge."
